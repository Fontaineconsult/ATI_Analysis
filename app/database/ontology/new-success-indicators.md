## Web

### Web Goal 1: Web Accessibility Evaluation Process

#### 1.20 — Establish a process to identify all campus-affiliated social media accounts and assess the accessibility of their shared content in alignment with recognized standards.

**Purpose.** The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure the institution is meeting the ADA Title II requirement that all institution-affiliated social media fundamentally meet WCAG 2.1 level AA.

**Examples of Evidence.** Evidence that this process exists and is being carried out could include:

- An inventory or registry of official campus-affiliated social media accounts.
- Policies or guidelines requiring new accounts to be registered centrally.
- Accessibility checklists for social media content (e.g., use of alt text, captions, hashtags, contrast, effective communication).
- Training materials or workshops for social media managers.
- Sample audit reports documenting accessibility findings on posts or campaigns.
- Documentation of outreach to account owners with accessibility recommendations.
- Workflows or checklists built into posting processes (e.g., reminders for alt text and captions).

**Example of Established Level.** At the *Established* level, the university has moved beyond ad hoc awareness and has a documented, consistent practice:

- **Position Descriptions:** A central communications or accessibility office is tasked with maintaining the account inventory. Trained staff are tasked with manual reviews of social media accounts.
- **Budget:** Resources exist for training, accessibility tools (e.g., captioning services, image description support), and audit support.
- **Process:** Documented procedures outline how accounts are identified, registered, and periodically assessed for content accessibility. Account owners are expected to comply with accessibility posting guidelines.
- **Output:** A current, centralized list of social media accounts exists, and reports from periodic content accessibility reviews are logged.

#### 1.21 — Develop a process to implement and regularly review accessibility statements in alignment with CSU Chancellor's Office guidance, ensuring statements are clearly posted on all campus-affiliated websites, applications, and digital platforms.

**Purpose.** The purpose of this success indicator is to periodically review accessibility statements and effective communication documents and follow current recommendations and guidance by the CSU Chancellor's Office.

**Examples of Evidence.** Evidence that this process is in place and working could include:

- Accessibility statement templates based on CSU guidance.
- Policy or procedure documents requiring statements on all websites, apps, and platforms.
- Centralized repository or inventory confirming which sites/platforms have statements posted.
- Screenshots or URLs showing statements visibly placed on digital properties.
- Communications to web administrators with instructions for posting and maintaining statements.
- Review schedules/logs documenting periodic checks for accuracy and compliance.
- Help desk or feedback records showing user reports submitted via posted statements.

**Example of Established Level.** At the *Established* level, the institution has moved beyond one-off efforts and maintains a standardized, enforced practice:

- **Position Descriptions & Responsibility:** Responsibility for accessibility statement review is assigned (e.g., accessibility office or central IT communications team).
- **Budget:** Resources allocated for staff time, compliance reviews, and technical support for statement updates.
- **Process:** Documented procedures define where statements must appear, who maintains them, and how they are reviewed (e.g., annually or bi-annually).
- **Output:** All campus-affiliated websites, apps, and platforms have an accessibility statement posted that aligns with CSU Chancellor's Office guidance.

### Web Goal 2: New Website/Web Application and Digital Content Design and Development Process

#### 2.10 — Establish a process for content editors to request an accessibility review of new web content, electronic documents, audio/video materials, and social media posts prior to publication, ensuring compliance with WCAG 2.1 AA standards.

**Purpose.** Ensure that accessibility issues are identified before publication, reducing remediation workload, legal risk, and user barriers. The goal is to move accessibility review from reactive remediation to a pre-publication quality control step.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Documented workflow for requesting accessibility review prior to publishing
- Defined intake form or ticketing process for review requests
- Published review criteria aligned with WCAG 2.1 AA
- Service-level expectations (e.g., review turnaround time)
- Communication to content editors about when review is required
- Tracking system for submitted and completed reviews
- Templates or checklists used during review
- Evidence that high-impact content requires review before release

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Responsibility for conducting accessibility reviews is formally assigned and documented in appropriate position descriptions.
- **Budget:** Dedicated staff time or workload allocation is identified to support ongoing accessibility review requests.
- **Procedures:** A complete, documented pre-publication accessibility review process aligned with WCAG 2.1 AA is required and consistently followed.
- **Output:** Accessibility review is a standard, tracked step prior to publishing high-impact digital content.

### Web Goal 3: Ongoing Monitoring Process

#### 3.7 — Develop a process that combines automated web accessibility scanning tools with manual testing methods to identify accessibility barriers.

**Purpose.** Ensure that barriers are identified through an evaluation process including manual verification as well as automated testing.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Defined schedule for automated scans (e.g., weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Manual testing protocols (e.g., keyboard-only testing checklist, screen reader testing guidance)
- Assigned staff responsible for conducting and reviewing scans, and cadence for manual reviews.
- Documentation showing findings are tracked and communicated for remediation
- Integration of results into remediation workflows or ticketing systems

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Designated accessibility staff (or web governance body) formally assigned responsibility for coordinating automated scans and manual testing activities.
- **Budget:** Institutional funding allocated for enterprise scanning tools and staff time to conduct manual evaluations.
- **Procedures:** Documented, campus-wide process outlining scan frequency, required manual testing methods (e.g., keyboard, screen reader, form testing), documentation standards, and remediation tracking workflow.
- **Output:** Regularly generated accessibility reports that combine automated scan results with documented manual testing findings and are distributed to responsible web owners for remediation.

#### 5.17 — Develop a process to provide accessible course syllabi at the beginning of each term and promote use of the university-approved LMS.

**Purpose.** The purpose of this indicator is to ensure students receive accessible course syllabi at the start of each term in a consistent and accessible format. **Examples of Evidence.** Campuses may demonstrate progress or achievement of this indicator through evidence such as:

- Syllabus accessibility guidelines or templates

- LMS course shell requirements or defaults
- Training on accessible syllabus creation

**Example of Established Level.** At the Established level, the campus has a consistent process for accessible syllabi delivery.

- Accessible syllabi are available at the beginning of each term
- Faculty use standardized templates or guidance
- Syllabi are posted within the university-approved LMS
- Accessibility expectations are clearly communicated
- The process supports early access and student success

### Instructional Materials Goal 6: Accessibility Requirements for Course Review and Remediation

#### 6.10 — The campus has established a process to evaluate the accessibility of Learning Tools Interoperability (LTI) integrations and external applications used within the campus Learning Management System (LMS), including publisher courseware and digital learning platforms.

**Purpose.** Ensure that third-party tools integrated into the LMS (e.g., LTIs, publisher platforms, external applications) are evaluated for accessibility before and during use. This indicator addresses a common risk area where inaccessible tools are introduced into courses, ensuring accessibility is considered in selection, integration, and ongoing use.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Documented process for evaluating accessibility of LTIs and external tools used in the LMS
- Accessibility review criteria aligned with Section 508 and WCAG 2.1 AA
- Requirement for vendors to provide accessibility documentation (e.g., VPAT/ACR)
- Collaboration between instructional design, procurement, and accessibility teams
- Defined workflow for reviewing and approving tools before use in courses
- Tracking of approved, conditionally approved, or restricted tools
- Guidance to faculty on selecting accessible tools and requesting reviews
- Integration with procurement or risk assessment processes where applicable

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Responsibility for evaluating the accessibility of LTIs and external LMS tools is formally assigned and documented across relevant roles.
- **Budget:** Identified staff time and resources support ongoing evaluation of third-party tools used in courses.
- **Procedures:** A complete, documented process requires accessibility evaluation of LTIs and external applications prior to approval and use, aligned with Section 508 and WCAG 2.1 AA.
- **Output:** Evidence showing that LMS-integrated tools are consistently reviewed, approved, and tracked based on accessibility, reducing the use of inaccessible third-party content.

## Procurement

### Procurement Goal 1: Procurement Processes

#### 1.12 — Develop a risk-informed process to evaluate Section 508 compliance for acquisitions, regardless of procurement method or cost (e.g., competitive and non-competitive bids, purchase card transactions, auxiliary, technology that students are required to purchase, LTIs, MEAs).

**Purpose.** The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that all ICT acquisitions, regardless of dollar value or purchasing channel, undergo an appropriate Section 508 accessibility review. This ensures consistent compliance, reduces risk, and establishes accountability across all purchasing activities, including purchasing or procurement card ("P-Card" or "pro-card") and auxiliary purchases. This new SI also aims to instruct campuses to include their auxiliaries, foundations, grant-based purchases, and related activities. All purchases affiliated with the university fall under the risk and compliance of the campus.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Written procedures or policies requiring accessibility review for all ICT acquisitions.
- Documentation of accessibility conformance processes/procedures at the procurement level.
- Communication outreach to campus auxiliaries, providing input on ICT procurement business processes.
- Policies or procedures for auxiliaries; procedures for inclusion of LTIs in Canvas that involve a review process.
- Accessibility review logs showing evaluations across procurement types (e.g., competitive bids, direct purchases, renewals, auxiliary purchases).
- Training records for P-Card holders and department buyers on accessibility compliance.
- Documentation showing accessibility review incorporated into purchase requisition or approval workflows.
- Reports summarizing Section 508 compliance rates across procurement methods.

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Procurement (both state-side and auxiliary) and ATI accessibility staff have defined responsibilities for reviewing ICT purchases, regardless of funding source or cost.
- **Budget:** Allocations exist for accessibility review staffing, tools, and training for decentralized buyers.
- **Procedures:** Documented procedures integrate accessibility checks into all procurement workflows across all university affiliates.
- **Output:** Comprehensive records show that accessibility review occurred for all ICT acquisitions.
- **Managed / Metrics:** Monitor acquisitions and determine if reviews are proceduralized for those other entities or free software.
- **Optimized / Administrative Review:** Leadership reviews audit results to ensure equitable application of Section 508 review across all procurement pathways.

### Procurement Goal 8: Experience/Implementation

#### 8.11 — The campus, including auxiliaries and affiliated entities, systematically identifies and addresses gaps across all procurement channels to ensure all acquired ICT undergoes accessibility validation.

**Purpose.** The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that accessibility review processes are applied consistently across all procurement channels, including those managed by auxiliaries, departments, or affiliated entities.

- Closes compliance gaps caused by decentralized purchasing practices.
- Ensures all ICT acquisitions, regardless of cost, funding source or buyer, are reviewed for accessibility.
- Promotes enterprise-wide accountability and alignment with CSU policy.
- Builds transparency and collaboration between central procurement, auxiliary organizations, and IT governance.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Documentation identifying all procurement channels and related workflows (e.g., P-Card, auxiliary, grants, direct purchases, no-cost acquisitions).
- Procedures or MOUs requiring auxiliary and affiliated entities to follow ATI procurement standards.
- Reports or audits identifying and tracking gaps in accessibility review coverage.
- Training materials and outreach communications for auxiliary buyers and managers.
- Meeting notes or correspondence showing collaboration between campus procurement, auxiliaries, and accessibility offices.
- Data showing increased participation or compliance from decentralized purchasing units.

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Roles responsible for oversight include the ATI Procurement Lead, Auxiliary Procurement Managers, and campus buyers, with duties reflected in job descriptions.
- **Budget:** Resources allocated for auxiliary training, outreach, and tracking tool integration.
- **Procedures:** A documented, campus-wide procedure ensures that all ICT acquisitions, regardless of funding source or cost, undergo accessibility review using the same evaluation criteria.
- **Output:** All purchasing pathways demonstrate compliance with ATI procurement processes, evidenced by audit logs and centralized reports.
- **Managed / Metrics:** Track percentage of ICT acquisitions reviewed across all channels, and measure reductions in unreviewed or unreported procurements.
- **Optimized / Administrative Review:** Annual reviews evaluate participation from auxiliary entities and document corrective actions where gaps are found.

#### 8.12 — Follow a platform-agnostic process to ensure that all ICT purchases undergo a consistent ATI accessibility conformance review, regardless of procurement method or system used.

**Purpose.** The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that ICT acquisitions made through the CSU Systemwide procurement platform (e.g., CSUBuy or other shared systems) receive the same accessibility review and approval as campus-managed procurements. Relying solely on CSUBuy P2P for workflow may fall short of established accessibility procurement framework standards. Campuses should ensure their process meets established standards:

- Consistent application of accessibility standards across all CSU purchasing mechanisms.
- Transparency and accountability for products acquired systemwide.
- Reduction of risk from accessibility gaps in large-scale or shared procurements.
- Collaboration between the campus and the Chancellor's Office procurement and accessibility teams.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Written procedures defining accessibility review workflows for systemwide purchases.
- Documentation showing accessibility review steps integrated into CSUBuy or equivalent systems.
- Cross-linking established forms or processes with CSUBuy.
- Training or communication materials ensuring campus buyers understand review expectations for systemwide acquisitions.
- Completed ACRs, TAAPs, or accessibility statements linked to systemwide contracts.

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Designated ATI Procurement Leads and system procurement liaisons are formally responsible for accessibility review coordination.
- **Budget:** Resources allocated for staff participation in systemwide review processes, tool licenses, and cross-campus collaboration.
- **Procedures:** Documented procedures ensure all ICT acquisitions through the CSU Systemwide platform undergo the same accessibility conformance checks as campus procurements.
- **Output:** Accessibility documentation (ACRs, TAAPs, Roadmaps) attached to each systemwide purchase record.
- **Managed / Metrics:** Track percentage of systemwide procurements reviewed for accessibility and time-to-completion of reviews.
- **Optimized / Administrative Review:** Annual review to assess compliance and identify opportunities for standardization or improvement.

- Role-specific onboarding content tailored to faculty, staff, student employees, and content creators

- **Procedures:** A documented process ensures accessibility awareness is consistently integrated into onboarding and orientation programs for all relevant audiences

- **Budget:** Resources are allocated to support communication efforts, including content development, platforms, and outreach activities
- **Procedures:** A documented communication process ensures that information about accessibility training, help, and resources is consistently distributed to all relevant content creators

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Budget:** Resources are allocated to support communication efforts and staff providing procurement-related accessibility assistance
- **Procedures:** A documented process ensures procurement stakeholders and vendors are consistently directed to appropriate accessibility contacts, resources, and support channels
- **Output:** Campus members and vendors involved in ICT procurement consistently know how to access accessibility support, resulting in more efficient and compliant procurement practices. Records or reports show that vendors and campus members are contacting the designated department or are directed appropriately.

## Governance, Planning, and Policies _(entirely new priority area)_

### Governance, Planning, and Policies Goal 1: Establish Clear Roles and Responsibilities for Accessibility Oversight

#### 1.1 — Assigned authority and responsibility for accessibility documented in appropriate position descriptions.

**Purpose.** Ensure that responsibility for digital accessibility is formally defined and embedded within institutional roles. Documenting accessibility expectations in position descriptions establishes accountability, clarifies ownership, and supports sustained implementation across all priority areas (Web, Instructional Materials, Procurement, and related functions).

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Position descriptions that explicitly include digital accessibility responsibilities (e.g., web content creators, instructional designers, procurement staff, IT leadership)
- Standard language or competencies related to accessibility included in HR classification or job templates
- Organizational charts or role matrices showing accessibility responsibilities across departments
- Performance evaluation criteria tied to accessibility-related duties
- Documentation aligning accessibility responsibilities with specific ATI priority areas or success indicators
- HR or governance policies requiring inclusion of accessibility in relevant roles

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** Accessibility responsibilities are clearly defined and formally included in relevant position descriptions across key roles (e.g., web, instructional materials, procurement, IT, communications)
- **Budget:** Funding supports roles with explicit accessibility responsibilities, including allocated time or dedicated positions
- **Procedures:** A standardized process ensures accessibility responsibilities are consistently incorporated into new and updated position descriptions through HR and governance workflows
- **Output:** Position descriptions across the institution consistently reflect assigned accessibility responsibilities, establishing clear accountability for implementation

#### 1.2 — Assigned authority and responsibility for the web evaluation process and ongoing monitoring (to include digital content) to a body (person(s) or business entity).

**Purpose.** Ensure that a clearly defined individual, team, or governance body is accountable for overseeing web accessibility evaluation and ongoing monitoring activities, including digital content. Establishing formal ownership supports coordination, consistency, and sustained compliance with accessibility standards across the institution.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Formal designation of a responsible body (e.g., ATI Committee, Digital Accessibility Team, IT unit, or designated coordinator)
- Organizational charts or governance documents identifying responsibility for web evaluation and monitoring
- Defined scope of responsibility that includes websites, web applications, and digital content (e.g., documents, multimedia)
- Role charters, committee descriptions, or service definitions outlining evaluation and monitoring duties
- Documented workflows showing how evaluation and monitoring activities are initiated, tracked, and reported
- Cross-functional coordination agreements between units (e.g., IT, communications, academic departments)

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** A specific individual or team is formally assigned responsibility for web accessibility evaluation and ongoing monitoring, including digital content
- **Budget:** Resources are allocated to support the responsible body, including staff time for evaluation and monitoring
- **Procedures:** A documented process defines how the responsible body conducts, coordinates, and maintains ongoing evaluation and monitoring activities across campus systems and content
- **Output:** Records or reports demonstrating that web accessibility evaluation and monitoring activities are consistently executed and coordinated by the assigned body, with clear institutional accountability

#### 1.3 — Assigned authority and responsibility for the new web/mobile development process (to include updates and maintenance) to a body (person(s) or business entity).

**Purpose.** Ensure that responsibility for accessible web and mobile development—across new builds, updates, and ongoing maintenance—is formally assigned to a designated individual, team, or governance body. Clear ownership enables consistent integration of accessibility throughout the development lifecycle and reduces the risk of introducing new barriers.

**Examples of Evidence.**

- Formal designation of a responsible body (e.g., central IT, web services, application development team, or digital accessibility program)
- Governance documentation or organizational charts identifying ownership of accessible development practices
- Defined scope covering new development, redesigns, updates, and maintenance activities
- Documented roles and responsibilities for developers, designers, product owners, and content contributors
- Development standards or guidelines that incorporate accessibility requirements (e.g., WCAG, Section 508)
- Workflows or lifecycle documentation showing how accessibility is integrated into design, development, testing, and deployment
- Coordination processes between development teams and accessibility specialists

**Example of Established Level.**

- **Position:** A designated individual or team is formally assigned responsibility for ensuring accessibility in web/mobile development, including updates and maintenance
- **Budget:** Resources are allocated to support accessible development practices, including staff time, tools, and training
- **Procedures:** A documented development lifecycle process ensures accessibility is consistently integrated into new builds, updates, and ongoing maintenance
- **Output:** Records or reports demonstrating that web and mobile applications are developed and maintained under a coordinated, accountable process led by the assigned body that consistently incorporates accessibility requirements

#### 1.4 — Assigned authority and responsibility for the accessible ICT procurement process to a body (person(s) or business entity).

**Purpose.** Ensure that a clearly defined individual, team, or governance body is accountable for overseeing the accessible ICT procurement process. Formal ownership establishes consistency in how accessibility is evaluated during purchasing, supports risk management, and ensures alignment with Section 508 and institutional requirements.
