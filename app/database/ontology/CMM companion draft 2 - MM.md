## Introduction

This document is a companion guide to the CSU Accessibility Technology Initiative (ATI) Capability Maturity Model. The purpose of this document is to provide guidance for specific ATI Goals and Success Criteria, to assist campuses in providing appropriate evidence and status levels that reflect their efforts.

## Status Level explanation

A maturity model is a process-improvement framework using a set of proven best practices that helps organizations understand their current level of capability and performance.  It also provides a guide for improvement.   A maturity model defines the capability of a given process. Each level in the model represents a stage of organizational progress, as a process develops and matures, moving from *ad hoc* practices toward standardized/defined, managed, and continuous improvement.

The goal of a maturity model is twofold:

1. Assessment – allows the organization to understand its current capabilities and performance.
1. Guidance – defines the capabilities associated with a given process and level.  The levels are progressing, demonstrating progress of improvement and maturity in each process.

Step for Completing the ATI Capability Maturity Model

1. Review Each Category and associated Processes – Review the processes being evaluated for each category.  Determine what evidence exists for each process by reviewing the columns (e.g., Position Descriptions, Budget, Process/Procedures, Output, Metrics, Review).
1. Identify the Maturity Level for Current Processes – For each process, identify which maturity level most closely matches the current state of that process:
  - *Not Started*: No activity or documentation.  No process exists.
  - *Initiated*: Early, tentative or ad hoc steps.
  - *Defined*: Practices are recognized but not codified.  Procedures may be informally documented.  Resources are not formally allocated.
  - *Established*: Processes are documented, and resources are consistently allocated.
  - *Managed*: Processes are measured, tracked, and tied to milestones.  Metrics exist for a given process.
  - *Optimizing*: Continuous improvement based on regular reviews and feedback.  Metrics are used for process improvement.
1. Select the Closest Fit – Choose the level that best aligns with the current state.  Key considerations are whether a process is documented, has staff and financial resources consistently allocated, whether metrics are collected and used, and whether the process is consistent for the enterprise.    
It is common for evaluators to feel that their process doesn’t exactly fit into just one level.  Evaluators are asked to make a “best effort,” even if your organization has some elements of multiple levels. ## Web Success Indicators

### Web Goal 1:  Web Accessibility Evaluation Process

#### Success indicator 1.2: Develop a process to inventory for all websites, applications (web or mobile) affiliated with the university, including those developed by contract vendors.

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure the university has a comprehensive and accurate record of all affiliated digital properties, including those created by vendors. This inventory:

- Provides visibility into the full digital ecosystem.
- Enables better resource allocation and risk management.
- Creates a foundation for ongoing monitoring, updates, and accountability.
##### Examples of evidence

- Policy or procedure documents describing how digital properties are reported and logged.
- Inventory database or registry (spreadsheet, system of record, or software tool).
- Contract language requiring vendors to disclose or register new applications.
- Change logs showing updates to the inventory when new sites/apps are added or decommissioned.
##### Example of an established level

- Position Descriptions: A designated office (e.g., central IT or Accessibility Office) is formally responsible for maintaining the inventory.
- Budget: Resources are allocated for inventory maintenance (e.g., staffing, software).
- Process: There is a documented procedure requiring all departments and vendor partners to register new sites and applications before launching.
- Output: A centralized, up-to-date inventory exists in a shared system accessible to stakeholders.

#### Success indicator 1.4: Develop a process to perform regularly scheduled accessibility evaluations to identify campus-affiliated websites and applications (web or mobile) that need remediation.

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that digital accessibility is not a one-time effort, but an ongoing practice.

##### Examples of evidence

Evidence that a process exists and is functioning could include:

- Evaluation schedules (e.g., monthly or quarterly automated scans, annual manual reviews).
- Accessibility audit reports (internal or vendor-conducted).
- Regular communication regarding automated reporting (e.g. Quarterly reports)
- Automated scan findings from tools such as Pope Tech, Level Access AMP, etc.
- Procedures or policies describing evaluation frequency and scope.
- Tracking logs showing issues identified, assigned, and resolved.
- Remediation plans are tied to specific findings.

##### Example of an established status level

At the *Established* level, the university has moved beyond ad hoc checks and conducts evaluations consistently and formally:

- Position Descriptions & Responsibilities: A designated office or position is formally responsible for scheduling and coordinating evaluations.
- Budget: Funding is allocated for accessibility tools (e.g., automated scanning platforms) and manual audits.
- Process/Procedures: Documented procedures define *who* conducts evaluations, *how often* they occur (e.g., annually, biannually), and *what methods* are used (automated scans, manual reviews, user testing) and how content owners are contacted about the results.
- Output: Evaluation reports are maintained, accessible to stakeholders, with clear findings and remediation recommendations.

#### Success Indicator 1.6: Develop a process to conduct manual accessibility evaluations on campus-affiliated websites and applications (web or mobile).

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that accessibility evaluations go beyond automated scans, which typically catch only 30–40% of issues. Manual evaluations:

- Identify complex barriers such as keyboard traps, improper use of ARIA, color contrast in context, and screen reader usability.
- Provide a deeper level of assurance that digital properties meet ADA And Section 508 standards.
- Support inclusive design by validating the real-world user experience, especially for people using assistive technologies.
- Strengthen institutional accountability by confirming accessibility at a human-centered level.
##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that a manual evaluation process exists and is functioning could include:

- Written procedures describing the scope and methods for manual testing (e.g., screen reader checks, keyboard navigation, zoom/text scaling).
- Qualifications of testers (e.g. WAS, DHS Trusted Tester)
- Evaluation templates or checklists used by testers.
- Training materials for staff performing manual reviews.
- Completed reports showing findings from manual audits.
- Logs or tickets documenting issues identified and remediation actions.
- Sample test plans including the use of multiple assistive technologies (JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, TalkBack, etc.).
##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, manual evaluations are not ad hoc but documented, consistent, and part of the institution’s accessibility practice:

- Position Descriptions: Staff roles (central accessibility office, IT accessibility lead, or trained testers) are trained and formally tasked with conducting manual evaluations.
- Budget: Resources allocated for training staff, purchasing assistive technology licenses, and/or contracting third-party evaluators.
- Process: Documented procedures specify when manual testing occurs (e.g., annually, prior to major site launches, or as part of procurement), which methods are used, and who is responsible. Output: Written evaluation reports exist for each review, with prioritized recommendations for remediation and are available to the responsible content owner.

#### Success Indicator 1.7 Develop a process to distribute evaluation results to campus members and/or vendors responsible for website maintenance.

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that accessibility evaluation results are shared with the right people who can act on them.

##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that this process exists and is functioning could include:

- Documented procedures for how evaluation results are shared (e.g., reports, dashboards, ticketing systems).
- Emails, memos, or notifications sent to site/application owners or vendors.
- Meeting notes or briefings where results are presented and remediation is discussed.
- Issue-tracking tickets tied directly to evaluation findings.
- Signed acknowledgments or confirmations from vendors or campus members that they received and reviewed results.
- Regular distribution schedules (e.g., results shared quarterly with leadership).

##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, the institution has a formal and consistent process for distributing evaluation results:

- Position Descriptions & Responsibility: Responsibility for distributing results is clearly assigned (e.g., accessibility office, IT service desk, procurement compliance).
- Budget: Resources may support communication tools, ticketing systems, or staff capacity for managing distribution.
- Process: Documented procedures define *who* receives results, *how* results are communicated (email, dashboard, shared reports, ticketing), and *when* (e.g., within 2 weeks of evaluation).
- Output: All campus-affiliated websites and applications evaluated have results shared with responsible parties (owners, maintainers, vendors).

#### Success Indicator 1.9 Ensure that campus members involved in maintaining web content (including social media, videos, electronic documents) and web/mobile applications understand the accessibility evaluation process

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to make sure that the people who actually create and maintain digital content (web editors, developers, content managers, social media managers, document authors, etc.) have enough knowledge of the evaluation process to act on results. This ensures:

- Accessibility responsibilities are shared with individual contributors
- Content creators understand how evaluation findings relate to their work.
- Remediation can happen more efficiently and accurately.
- Accessibility becomes part of daily workflows rather than an afterthought.

##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that this is happening could include:

- Training materials or sessions that explain evaluation methods, findings, and remediation expectations.
- Attendance logs showing participation of content owners, editors, or developers.
- Guidelines or handbooks provided to campus members and vendors.
- Post-training assessments or surveys confirming understanding.
- Help desk or support tickets where content maintainers requested clarification and received guidance.
- Meeting agendas/notes where evaluation processes were discussed with content owners.
- Communications (memos, newsletters, email reminders) that highlight evaluation steps and roles.

##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, the institution has moved beyond ad hoc awareness-building and has a formal, repeatable program to ensure understanding:

- Position Descriptions & Responsibility: Content maintainers are expected (and sometimes required) to participate in training or orientation about the accessibility evaluation process.
- Budget: Funds allocated for training development, workshops, or external trainers.
- Process: A documented training/onboarding program explains the evaluation process and how findings are communicated and resolved. Sessions are scheduled regularly and required for key roles.
- Output: Majority of staff and vendors who maintain websites, applications, and digital content have completed training or certification.

#### Success Indicator 1.19 Develop a process for application-specific accessibility statements for third party products and platforms in use that have known accessibility barriers

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure that university provided third party products and platforms provide accessibility statements and effective communication methods on all digital assets.

##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that this process is in place and working could include:

- Procurement policies requiring an accessibility statement and appropriate elevation of response as necessary.
- Policy or Design documents including an Accessibility statement based on CSU template.
- Centralized repository or inventory confirming which sites/platforms have statements posted.
- Communications to web administrators with instructions for posting and maintaining statements.
- Help desk or feedback records showing user reports submitted via posted statements.

##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, the university has gone beyond awareness and put a formalized, consistent process in place:

- Position Descriptions & Responsibility: Responsibility is formally assigned (e.g., accessibility office in collaboration with IT procurement and application owners).
- Budget: Allocations exist for staff time to draft and update statements, and possibly for legal/compliance review.
- Process: A documented procedure requires creating an application-specific accessibility statement whenever a third-party product with known accessibility barriers is adopted. Statements include vendor information, description of barriers, known workarounds, and contact information for support.
- Output: A collection of standardized accessibility statements is maintained and accessible to campus members.

#### Success Indicator 1.20 NEW Establish a process to identify all campus-affiliated social media accounts and assess the accessibility of their shared content in alignment with recognized standards.

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to ensure the institution is meeting the ADA Title II requirement that all institution affiliated Social Media fundamentally meet WCAG 2.1 level AA.

##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that this process exists and is being carried out could include:

- An inventory or registry of official campus-affiliated social media accounts.
- Policies or guidelines requiring new accounts to be registered centrally.
- Accessibility checklists for social media content (e.g., use of alt text, captions, hashtags, contrast, effective communication).
- Training materials or workshops for social media managers.
- Sample audit reports documenting accessibility findings on posts or campaigns.
- Documentation of outreach to account owners with accessibility recommendations.
- Workflows or checklists built into posting processes (e.g., reminders for alt text and captions).

##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, the university has moved beyond ad hoc awareness and has a documented, consistent practice:

- Position Descriptions: A central communications or accessibility office is tasked with maintaining the account inventory. Trained staff are tasked with manual reviews of social media accounts.
- Budget: Resources exist for training, accessibility tools (e.g., captioning services, image description support), and audit support.
- Process: Documented procedures outline how accounts are identified, registered, and periodically assessed for content accessibility. Account owners are expected to comply with accessibility posting guidelines.
- Output: A current, centralized list of social media accounts exists, and reports from periodic content accessibility reviews are logged.

#### Success Indicator 1.21 NEW Develop a process to implement and regularly review accessibility statements in alignment with CSU Chancellor’s Office guidance, ensuring statements are clearly posted on all campus-affiliated websites, applications, and digital platforms

##### Purpose

The purpose of this success indicator is to periodically review accessibility statements and effective communication documents and follow current recommendations and guidance by the CSU Chancellor’s Office

##### Examples of Evidence

Evidence that this process is in place and working could include:

- Accessibility statement templates based on CSU guidance.
- Policy or procedure documents requiring statements on all websites, apps, and platforms.
- Centralized repository or inventory confirming which sites/platforms have statements posted.
- Screenshots or URLs showing statements visibly placed on digital properties.
- Communications to web administrators with instructions for posting and maintaining statements.
- Review schedules/logs documenting periodic checks for accuracy and compliance.
- Help desk or feedback records showing user reports submitted via posted statements.

##### Example of Established Level

At the *Established* level, the institution has moved beyond one-off efforts and maintains a standardized, enforced practice:

- Position Descriptions & Responsibility: Responsibility for accessibility statement review is assigned (e.g., accessibility office or central IT communications team).
- Budget: Resources allocated for staff time, compliance reviews, and technical support for statement updates.
- Process: Documented procedures define where statements must appear, who maintains them, and how they are reviewed (e.g., annually or bi-annually).
- Output: All campus-affiliated websites, apps, and platforms have an accessibility statement posted that aligns with CSU Chancellor’s Office guidance.

### Web Goal 2: New Website/Web Application and Digital Content Design and Development Process

#### Success Indicator 2.1 Develop a process to ensure that accessibility is integrated at all stages of the website/application and mobile development lifecycle, including requirements-gathering, design, development, testing, and deployment, to meet Section 508 and WCAG standards

##### Purpose

Ensure accessibility is considered by internal developers — not added retroactively.

This applies to campus-developed or campus-directed web and mobile applications, including coding, configuration, and technical implementation.

##### Examples of Evidence

- Documented SDLC that includes accessibility checkpoints
- Accessibility requirements in project intake forms
- Coding standards referencing WCAG
- Accessibility defects tracked in ticketing systems
- QA sign-off includes accessibility verification
- Vendor development contracts reference Section 508/WCAG
##### Example of Established Level

Position Descriptions: Accessibility responsibilities are identified in developer and technical lead position descriptions.

Budget: Budget allocated for continuing education including Accessibility SMEs and developers.

Process: A complete, documented development process includes required accessibility checkpoints. Accessibility validation is a standard, formal release requirement.

Output: Accessibility defects are tracked and remediated before production.

#### Success Indicator 2.10 NEW Establish a process for content editors to request an accessibility review of new web content, electronic documents, audio/video materials, and social media posts prior to publication, ensuring compliance with WCAG 2.1 AA standards.

##### Purpose

Ensure that accessibility issues are identified before publication, reducing remediation workload, legal risk, and user barriers. The goal is to move accessibility review from reactive remediation to a pre-publication quality control step.

##### Examples of Evidence

- Documented workflow for requesting accessibility review prior to publishing
- Defined intake form or ticketing process for review requests
- Published review criteria aligned with WCAG 2.1 AA
- Service-level expectations (e.g., review turnaround time)
- Communication to content editors about when review is required
- Tracking system for submitted and completed reviews
- Templates or checklists used during review
- Evidence that high-impact content requires review before release
##### Example of Established Level

- Position: Responsibility for conducting accessibility reviews is formally assigned and documented in appropriate position descriptions.
- Budget: Dedicated staff time or workload allocation is identified to support ongoing accessibility review requests.
- Procedures: A complete, documented pre-publication accessibility review process aligned with WCAG 2.1 AA is required and consistently followed.
- Output: Accessibility review is a standard, tracked step prior to publishing high-impact digital content.

### Web Goal 3: Ongoing Monitoring Process

#### Success Indicator 3.2 Develop a process to verify that any changes made to existing websites, web applications, mobile apps, electronic documents, and audio and video comply with Section 508 accessibility standards.

##### Purpose

Ensure that accessibility is maintained over time by verifying that updates, edits, patches, redesigns, and content changes do not introduce new accessibility barriers.

This indicator focuses on change management and ongoing maintenance, ensuring accessibility compliance is preserved after initial development or remediation.

##### Examples of Evidence

- Documented procedure requiring accessibility verification when digital content or systems are updated
- Accessibility checkpoints embedded in change management workflows
- Requirement for accessibility testing prior to publishing updates
- Defined criteria for what types of changes trigger review
- Ticketing or tracking system that includes accessibility validation
- Communication to content owners and developers regarding update responsibilities

##### Example of Established Level

- Position: Responsibility for verifying accessibility during updates is formally assigned and documented in appropriate roles.
- Budget: Identified staff time or workload allocation supports ongoing accessibility verification activities.
- Procedures: A complete, documented process requires accessibility validation as part of change management for digital updates.
- Output: Accessibility verification is a standard, tracked step before publishing changes to existing digital assets.

#### Success Indicator 3.7 NEW Develop a process that combines automated web accessibility scanning tools with manual testing methods to identify accessibility barriers.

##### Purpose

Ensure that barriers are identified through an evaluation process including manual verification as well as automated testing.

##### Examples of Evidence

- Defined schedule for automated scans (e.g., weekly, monthly, quarterly)
- Manual testing protocols (e.g., keyboard-only testing checklist, screen reader testing guidance) a
- Assigned staff responsible for conducting and reviewing scans, and cadence for manual reviews.
- Documentation showing findings are tracked and communicated for remediation
- Integration of results into remediation workflows or ticketing systems

##### Example of Established Level

- Position: Designated accessibility staff (or web governance body) formally assigned responsibility for coordinating automated scans and manual testing activities.
- Budget: Institutional funding allocated for enterprise scanning tools and staff time to conduct manual evaluations.
- Procedures: Documented, campus-wide process outlining scan frequency, required manual testing methods (e.g., keyboard, screen reader, form testing), documentation standards, and remediation tracking workflow.
- Output: Regularly generated accessibility reports that combine automated scan results with documented manual testing findings and are distributed to responsible web owners for remediation.

### Web Goal 4: Evaluate Digital Content & Electronic Documents Held on the Web

## Instructional Materials Success Indicators

### Instructional Materials Goal 1:  Timely Adoption of Instructional Materials

#### Success Indicator 1.2: Develop a process (e.g., developed and documented practices, specified staff time, educational/training resources, and technology) to achieve compliance with timely adoption.

##### Purpose:

The purpose of this indicator is to ensure the campus has a defined, repeatable, and documented process that supports the timely adoption of instructional materials in compliance with applicable accessibility requirements. This includes clearly assigning responsibility, allocating sufficient staff time, providing appropriate training and guidance, and using tools or systems that enable early identification, review, and remediation of instructional materials prior to course delivery.

A well-established process ensures instructional materials are in place for early registration and accommodation workflows, reduces last-minute remediation, improves student access from the first day of instruction, and supports sustainable compliance across academic terms.

##### Examples of Evidence

Campuses may demonstrate progress or achievement of this indicator through evidence such as:

- A documented instructional materials adoption workflow outlining roles, timelines, and decision points
- Written procedures or guidance for faculty and departments on adoption deadlines
- Defined staff roles responsible for coordinating adoption and accessibility review
- Allocation of dedicated staff time for instructional materials review and coordination
- Training materials, workshops, or onboarding resources for faculty and instructional designers
- Use of technology or systems such as LMS reporting, adoption tracking tools, or curriculum management platforms
- Communications or reminders sent to faculty regarding adoption deadlines and accessibility requirements
- Records or statistics showing early adoption timelines aligned with accessibility review cycles available through campus bookstore platforms or other methods.

##### Example of Established Level

At the Established level, the campus has implemented and operationalized a documented process to support the timely adoption of instructional materials.

- Position Description
- Budget
- Process/Procedures: The campus maintains and enforces a written, campuswide process that defines how and when instructional materials are adopted, and accounts for courses where instructors are added late by auto-adopting textbooks.
- Output
- Roles and responsibilities are clearly assigned across academic departments, the library, instructional design, accessibility, ATI teams, bookstores, etc.
- Faculty are informed of adoption timelines that allow sufficient time for accessibility review and remediation prior to the start of instruction
- Staff time is explicitly allocated to manage adoption coordination and accessibility review activities
- Training and guidance are regularly provided to faculty on accessible instructional materials and adoption expectations
- Technology or tracking mechanisms are used to monitor adoption status and identify risks early
- The process is used consistently across terms and is reviewed periodically for effectiveness and improvement
- Communication from Provost, Deans, or Chairs is consistent and aligns with term planning and other dependencies.
#### Success Indicator 1.6 Develop a process to distribute timely adoption performance reports to campus administration each semester.

##### Purpose: The purpose of this indicator is to ensure campus leadership has timely, actionable visibility into instructional materials adoption practices and related accessibility risks. Regular reporting supports accountability, informs resource allocation, and enables administrative leaders to identify trends, gaps, and areas requiring intervention to ensure timely adoption.

##### Examples of Evidence

Campuses may demonstrate progress or achievement of this indicator through evidence such as:

- A documented reporting process or schedule for instructional materials adoption
- Standardized report templates or dashboards showing adoption timelines and compliance metrics
- Defined roles responsible for compiling and distributing reports
- Semester-based reports shared with deans, provost offices, or academic leadership
- Use of LMS data, bookstore adoption systems, or tracking tools to generate reports
- Communications or meeting materials demonstrating administrative review of reports
- Documentation showing follow-up actions based on report findings

##### Example of Established Level

At the Established level, the campus has a consistent, documented process for producing and distributing instructional materials and adoption performance reports each semester.

- Reports are generated on a predictable schedule aligned with academic terms
- Reports include meaningful metrics related to timely adoption and accessibility readiness
- Campus administration receives reports in time to take corrective or supportive action
- Responsibilities for report creation, review, and distribution are clearly defined
- Reports are used to inform planning, resourcing, and continuous improvement efforts

### Instructional Materials Goal 4: Instructor Use of LMS (or non-LMS) Course Websites

#### Success Indicator 4.1 Develop a process to promote the posting of accessible instructional materials to the university approved LMS.

##### Purpose: To encourage consistent use of the university-approved LMS as the primary platform for delivering accessible instructional materials. Centralizing content within the LMS supports accessibility review, accommodation workflows, and consistent student access while reducing reliance on inaccessible or unsupported external platforms.

##### Examples of Evidence:

- Campuses may demonstrate progress or achievement of this indicator through evidence such as:
- Policies or guidance encouraging use of the university approved LMS

#### Important note regarding VPATs and HECVATs

These documents are another data point; they have value, but keep in mind:

- VPATs (ACRs) are self-reported — they are vendor claims, not independently verified findings.
  - ACRs authored by independent third parties with established accessibility credentials (such as IAAP certification) are typically higher quality.
- Author expertise varies wildly, from accessibility experts to non-technical staff.
- They are often treated as marketing collateral, not technical assessments.
- Legal review frequently prioritizes risk mitigation over accuracy.
- They are commonly out of date relative to the current product version.
- They are not comparable across vendors due to inconsistent interpretation and depth.
- Multiple formats and versions reduce consistency and reliability.
- They disclose only what vendors choose to report, omitting many real barriers.
- Vendors have financial incentives to overstate accessibility.
- They do not validate real-world usability or task completion.
- Accessibility demonstration scripts or testing templates.
- Correspondence showing follow-up with vendors regarding accessibility clarifications.
- Documentation of accessibility testing conducted to confirm vendor claims.
- Training materials for procurement staff on interpreting and validating ACRs.

NDAs

- Version-controlled planning documents reflecting accessibility goals, updates, and progress
- Integration points between the ATI Plan, IT Strategic Plan, and broader campus strategic plan
- Communication or reporting mechanisms to share approved plans with campus stakeholders

##### Example of Established Level

- Position: The ATI Steering Committee and subcommittees are formally assigned responsibility for reviewing, revising, and approving accessibility-related plans
- Budget: Resources are allocated to support planning activities, including staff time, coordination, and tools for documentation and collaboration
- Procedures: A documented, recurring process defines how plans are reviewed, updated, and approved, including timelines, roles, and decision-making protocols
- Output: Accessibility is consistently incorporated into institutional planning documents, with formally reviewed and approved plans guiding campus efforts

## Glossary

ACR (Accessibility Conformance Report)

A completed accessibility report, typically based on a VPAT, describing how a product meets accessibility standards.

ad hoc - refers to a method or approach created for a specific purpose or situation, as well as informal or unplanned. It is not part of an organization's standard procedures.

ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act) Title II

Federal regulation requiring public institutions, including universities, to provide equal access to programs, services, and digital content.

ATI (Accessibility Technology Initiative)

CSU (California State University) systemwide initiative focused on accessibility across web, instructional materials, procurement, and related areas.

Auxiliary

Campus-affiliated organization (e.g., foundation, bookstore) that may operate outside standard procurement workflows but is still subject to accessibility requirements.

Digital Content

Information provided electronically, including websites, documents, multimedia, social media, and instructional materials.

HR Human Resources

The campus unit(s) responsible for position descriptions, hiring, classification, and performance management, including integrating accessibility responsibilities into roles and supporting workforce training and compliance initiatives.

ICT (Information and Communication Technology)

All technology used to create, store, display, or transmit information, including software, hardware, applications, and digital content.

Instructional Materials

Course-related content such as syllabi, documents, textbooks, multimedia, courseware, and LMS content.

LMS (Learning Management System)

The university-approved platform used to deliver and manage instructional content and course activities.

LTI (Learning Tools Interoperability)

A standard that enables external tools and applications to integrate with the LMS.

Manual Evaluation

Accessibility testing performed by a human reviewer using methods such as keyboard navigation, screen readers, and usability checks.

MEA (Master Enabling Agreement)

A CSU Chancellor’s Office agreement that campuses can use for systemwide procurement.

Procurement Card (P-Card / Pro-card)

A campus-issued credit card used for decentralized purchasing.

Remediation

The process of correcting accessibility barriers in digital content or technology.

Section 508

Federal accessibility requirements governing ICT, commonly used as a compliance benchmark in higher education.

TAAP (Temporary Alternate Access Plan)

A documented plan describing how access will be provided when a product has known accessibility barriers.

VPAT (Voluntary Product Accessibility Template)

The standard template vendors use to report accessibility conformance, resulting in an ACR when completed.

WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines)

International standards for digital accessibility.

WCAG 2.1 AA

The current commonly required level of WCAG conformance for CSU digital accessibility.
