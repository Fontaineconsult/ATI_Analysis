The candidates, by what they signal
Compliance Management System (CMS) or GRC platform (Governance, Risk, and Compliance). This is the most literal fit for the surface description — you're tracking compliance with legal obligations, evidence, and accountability. GRC is a term every executive and auditor knows. The downside: GRC connotes a defensive, checkbox, audit-survival posture. It frames the work as cost-and-risk-avoidance, which undersells what you've built and tends to attract minimal "keep us out of trouble" funding rather than strategic investment. If you want it seen as a necessary evil, call it GRC.
Knowledge Management System (KMS). Captures the part GRC misses — that you're encoding institutional knowledge (the framework, the principles, the determinations, the descriptions) so it persists beyond individuals. Executives understand KM as a hedge against knowledge walking out the door. But KM alone undersells the operational and compliance teeth; it sounds like a wiki or a SharePoint, passive reference rather than an active system of record.
System of Record (SoR). This is a strong, precise executive term and probably closer than the first two. A system of record is the authoritative source for a domain of data — when there's a question about accessibility compliance status, this is where the answer lives, definitively. Executives treat systems of record as infrastructure worth investing in, because they become load-bearing. The connotation is authority and permanence, which matches what you're building. The limate: SoR describes its status (authoritative) more than its function (what it does).
Program Management / PPM (Project and Portfolio Management). Captures the implementation-and-work-tracking side — plans, implementations, the working teams, the dimensional coverage. Executives know PPM as how strategic initiatives get tracked and resourced. But PPM connotes generic task-tracking (the Asana risk from earlier), and it misses the compliance grounding and the knowledge layer entirely.
Maturity Model / Capability Management. Given that the CSU ATI is a capability-maturity framework and you're now grounding in the W3C AMM too, "accessibility maturity management" is accurate and has real executive traction — maturity models are a language leadership already speaks (CMMI, etc.), and "moving the organization up the maturity curve" is a fundable narrative. This captures the trajectory-and-improvement spine well. It undersells the system-of-record and knowledge aspects, though.
The term I'd actually reach for
What you've built doesn't fit any single one cleanly because it's genuinely three things fused: a compliance system of record, a knowledge management layer, and a maturity/program-management engine. But if I had to pick the one executive-directed term that captures the whole and positions it well, it's:
A compliance system of record — or, more completely, an accessibility governance and compliance system of record.
Here's the reasoning. "System of record" is the part that signals authority and infrastructure — this is where the truth about our accessibility posture lives, definitively, which is exactly what a knowledge graph grounding work to governance and evidence is. "Governance and compliance" names the domain in terms leadership and auditors immediately understand. Together they say: this is the authoritative source of truth for whether and how we meet our accessibility obligations. That framing gets infrastructure-level treatment rather than checkbox-tool treatment, and it's accurate.
The reason I'd resist plain "GRC platform" even though it's the closest off-the-shelf label: GRC tools are a known commercial category (ServiceNow, Archer, etc.), and calling yours a GRC platform invites the question "why aren't we just buying one." What you've built is domain-specific in a way generic GRC isn't — it encodes the accessibility framework, the legal reasoning, the maturity model, the interface ontology. So "system of record" positions it as the authoritative source for this domain rather than as an instance of a category leadership might prefer to procure.
The framing that captures the differentiator
If you want one sentence to hand an executive, the thing that distinguishes this from any of the off-the-shelf categories is that it doesn't just store compliance data — it reasons in the institution's own terms. So:
"An accessibility compliance system of record that encodes not just what we've done, but the legal obligations, institutional framework, and organizational maturity behind it."
That sentence does the positioning: "system of record" gets it taken seriously as infrastructure; "compliance" names the domain; and the clause after the comma is what justifies why it's bespoke rather than procured — it carries the reasoning, which generic tools don't.
A caution on over-claiming
One thing to be careful of with executives: the meta-layer (principles, determinations, the executable-heuristic ambitions) is intellectually the most interesting part, but it's also the part most likely to sound like over-engineering to a CFO if led with. I'd position the system primarily as a compliance system of record with a maturity-tracking engine — both of which map to obligations leadership already feels (audit-readiness, the 2027 Title II deadline, OCR exposure) — and treat the knowledge/reasoning layer as what makes the system of record trustworthy and durable rather than as a feature in its own right. The framework and principles are the why it's reliable, not the headline. Leadership funds reliable compliance infrastructure; they're warier of funding an ontology.
So: the common executive-directed term is system of record (in the governance/compliance domain), and the one-line frame is an accessibility compliance system of record that carries the obligations and maturity behind the data, not just the data. That positions it as fundable infrastructure while staying honest about what it actually is.
If you tell me the audience more specifically — a CFO, a provost, a CIO, an audit committee — I can sharpen the term and the framing, because each of those files it slightly differently (a CIO hears "system of record" as architecture, an audit committee hears "compliance" as exposure-reduction, a provost hears "maturity" as institutional capability).
