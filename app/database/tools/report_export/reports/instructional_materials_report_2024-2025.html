📝 Notes (1)
2024-12-05 - Angie Petty

An Excerpt from a PD page in the Excellence in Online Pedagogies Course- https://sfsu.instructure.com/courses/46763 Designing for an Online Course Adapted from: Quality Matters. (2023, October). Bridge to quality: A QM online course design guide basic edition. Retrieved from: https://qualitymatters.org/higher-ed-bridge-guide-basic When choosing and creating course content: 1 Prioritize academic integrity by clearly identifying the source material and adhering to copyright law. 2 Ensure content variety, including different types of materials beyond textbooks, reflecting current thought in the field. 3 Ensure easy accessibility of all materials, such as short videos with clear audio and closed captions, and readable text. 4 Proactively meet accessibility requirements for all content, focusing on already accessible materials and creating accessible content when necessary. Course materials should be designed to facilitate readability and minimize distractions. This involves breaking up large blocks of text, using headings for organization, and ensuring appropriate font size, type, formatting, and color contrast. Avoiding excessive use of color or purely decorative images is recommended to prevent cognitive distractions. Additionally, text alignment and use of multimedia controls should be optimized for accessibility and ease of use. For multimedia content, educators should prioritize good audio quality, shorter video length, and clear resolution to maintain visibility of playback controls. Interactive elements should allow learners to control motion and navigation features. Providing accessible text-based materials is crucial for supporting the success of all online learners and ensuring compliance with accessibility policies. Educators should use the Canvas Accessibility Checker tool to check the accessibility of text-based materials and to review the accessibility of publisher materials. For additional guidance please review the Disability Programs and Resource Center’s (DPRC) website: https://access.sfsu.edu/ Consider a holistic view of technology usage, avoiding reliance on a single tool and considering pedagogical fit when selecting technologies. Multimedia should be used effectively, with clear audio and non-distracting visual elements, and synchronous technologies should be accessible to all learners.

Databases and Electronic Resources: Accessibility features vary by database, depending on what the database vendor/publisher has put in place. When acquiring new databases and electronic collections, vendors are required to submit a VPAT or accessibility statement outlining how they make materials accessible. We review the evaluation methods they describe, look at conformance levels, and assess each product to determine whether their conformance will meet our needs. We do not follow strict guidelines for what is considered acceptable conformance because each database varies in terms of the content and retrieval methods, but we are generally looking to ensure content can be accessed at the “supported” or “partially supported” conformance levels.
